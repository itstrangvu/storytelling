01
You
Before the story, there's a narrator. Start there.
Before you begin

Introduce
yourself
first

— / 4
Name + role
One relevant detail
Not your full résumé. Just enough to anchor where your perspective comes from. "I work on checkout flows and have shipped 3 redesigns in 2 years." Earn trust in the first 30 seconds.
Connect
Why do you care about this?
Briefly explain why storytelling matters to you. It signals authenticity and frames everything coming next. Without it, you're just a voice delivering content — not a person with a point of view.
Source
Where does the story come from?
Real projects. Lived experience. Things that worked — or didn't. Failure stories land harder than success stories. They're rarer, more honest, and people remember them longer.
"If they don't know you, they can't trust your story."
This is the whole game before a single slide loads.
02
Them
Your story lives in their heads. Design for it.
Principle 01

Think about
your
audience

— / 4
Background
What do they already know?
Their expertise shapes how deep you can go from slide one. Don't explain Figma components to a room of senior product designers — you'll lose them in the first minute.
References
Who do they admire?
"Similar to how Linear approaches motion..." or "Like what the Duolingo team does with empty states..." — abstract ideas become concrete fast when grounded in work they already love.
Unknown crowd
What if you don't know them?
Ask. Open with "Quick show of hands — who here has ever..." and tailor the rest on the fly. Improvisation is a design skill. The room will trust you more for asking than for assuming.
Language
Use language they already use
Tokens, component libraries, user journeys — whatever lives in their daily vocabulary. Shared language builds connection. Exclusive jargon builds walls. Never make them feel behind.

Scope
Micro or macro — say it out loud
Are you telling a micro story (one design decision) or a macro story (a full project arc)? Both are valid. Mixing them without signalling the shift confuses the room. Decide upfront and name it.
Navigation
Show the page count
"Slide 4 of 12" gives people an anchor. They can reference back later: "Can we go to slide 3?" It tells them how much is left, reduces anxiety, and makes Q&A far cleaner for everyone.

Principle 03 & 04

Show,

— / 3
The contrast
Telling vs. showing
Telling
  • The navigation was confusing
  • Users struggled to complete tasks
  • We improved the experience
  • The new design performs better
VS
Showing
  • Play the session recording where they get stuck
  • "73% couldn't find checkout on mobile"
  • Before and after side by side
  • CSAT up 18 points after launch
Format
Choose your delivery format
High engagement, high risk
People lean in when it's live. Always have a backup — screenshot deck or recorded walkthrough.
If it breaks — keep moving
"Let me show you this instead." Never apologise for more than five seconds. Composure is the skill.
Narrate what you're doing
People follow your voice, not your cursor. Tell them what to look at and why it matters.
Slides are the backdrop
You are the story. Dense slides compete with your voice — and they always win badly.
Build to the reveal
Don't front-load the punchline. Establish the problem, let the tension sit, then show the solution.
Let them get excited
Curiosity is a hook. Give people something to wonder about before you give them the answer.
Context → live → debrief
Set up in slides, go live, return to slides to reflect. Structure without sacrificing spontaneity.
Signal every transition
"I'm jumping into the product for a second." Say it out loud. Format surprises break attention.
Excitement
Let them get excited
Don't give away the end at the beginning. Establish the problem vividly. Let the tension sit. Then reveal. Pause after key moments — silence isn't awkward, it's emphasis. Designers know this from animation. Apply it to speaking.
05
Feel it
You think visually. Now think emotionally.
Principle 05

Lighten
the
mood

— / 4
Opening
One light moment
A funny quirk from the process, a relatable failure, or a clever observation. It disarms the room before the substance begins. Don't force it — the room will feel it either way.
Recovery
What if the joke doesn't land?
"I thought that was funnier in my head" — then move on. Owning it is more charming than explaining it. Never over-recover. Five seconds of acknowledgment, then continue.
Cognitive load
Don't make it a mental exercise
If people are holding too many threads, simplify. Complexity is your problem to solve — not theirs to endure. A story should flow, not feel like a puzzle they're being asked to assemble.
"Match the energy you want back. Designers read signal as fast as anyone."
Vary your pace. Pause for effect. Invite a question. A curious audience is an engaged one.

Words
The best storytellers don't show off their vocabulary. They borrow yours.
Principle 06

Speak
their
language

— / 3
Vocabulary
Use terms they already use
Figma, design tokens, component libraries, user journeys — whatever lives in their daily vocabulary. It signals you're one of them. People trust speakers who live in the same conceptual world.
References
Name the work they admire
"Similar to how Linear approaches motion..." or "Like what the Duolingo team does with empty states..." Abstract ideas become concrete instantly when grounded in work they already respect.
Jargon
Avoid the jargon trap
If you're not sure they know a term, define it — briefly, without condescension. Shared language builds connection. Exclusive jargon builds walls. Never make them feel behind for not knowing something.
