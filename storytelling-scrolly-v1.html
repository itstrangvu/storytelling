The art of
telling the story

Every design decision has a story behind it. Let's learn how to tell it in a way that actually lands.

01
You
Before the story, there's a narrator. And that's you.
Before you begin

Introduce
yourself first

"If they don't know you, they can't trust your story. Earn that trust in the first 30 seconds."
Name + role

One relevant detail

Not your full résumé. Just enough to anchor where your perspective comes from. "I work on checkout flows and have shipped 3 redesigns in 2 years."

Connect

Why do you care?

Briefly explain why storytelling matters to you. It signals authenticity and frames everything that's coming next.

Source

Where does the story come from?

Real projects. Lived experience. Things that worked or didn't. The best stories have a source you can point to.

Scope

Set the expectations

A micro story (one decision) or a macro story (full arc)? Signal the scope upfront. Mixing them without a transition loses the room.

02
Them
Your story lives in their heads. Design for it.
Principle 01

Think about
your audience

01
What do they already know?
Their background and domain expertise shape how deep you can go from slide one. Don't explain Figma components to a room of senior product designers.
02
Who do they admire?
Reference projects, designers, or products they respect. "Similar to how Linear approaches motion..." — abstract ideas become concrete instantly.
03
What if you don't know them?
Ask. Open with "Quick show of hands — who here has..." and tailor the rest on the fly. Improvisation is a design skill too.
04
Use language they already use
Tokens, component libraries, user journeys — use what lives in their daily vocabulary. Shared language builds connection. Exclusive jargon builds walls.

04
Show it
A screenshot says more than a paragraph.
Principle 03 & 04

Show, don't
tell

Telling
  • The navigation was confusing
  • Users struggled to complete tasks
  • We improved the experience
  • The new design performs better
VS
Showing
  • Play the session recording where they get stuck
  • "73% couldn't find checkout on mobile"
  • Before and after side by side
  • CSAT up 18 points after launch
High risk

High engagement

People lean in when it's live. But always have a backup — a recorded walkthrough or a clickable prototype.

If it breaks

Keep moving

"Let me show you this instead" — then continue. Never apologise for more than five seconds. Composure is the skill.

Structure

Slides are the backdrop

You are the story. Dense slides compete with your voice — and they always win badly.

Reveal

Build to the punchline

Don't front-load the answer. Establish the problem vividly, let the tension sit, then show the solution.

Context → live → debrief

Set up the story in slides, demonstrate it live, then return to slides to reflect. Structure without sacrificing spontaneity. Signal transitions out loud: "I'm going to jump into the product for a second."

05
Feel it
You already think visually. Now think emotionally.
Principle 05

Lighten
the mood

"Match the energy you want back. This is a room of designers — they read signal as fast as anyone."
Open

One light moment

A funny quirk from the process, a relatable fail, or a clever observation. It disarms the room before the substance begins.

Recovery

If the joke doesn't land

"I thought that was funnier in my head" — then move on. Owning it is more charming than explaining it. Never over-recover.

Cognitive load

Don't make it a mental exercise

Complexity is your problem to solve, not theirs to endure. If they're holding too many threads at once, simplify.

Excitement

Let them get excited

Vary your pace. Pause for effect. Raise an eyebrow. Invite a question mid-way. A curious audience is an engaged audience.

06
Words
The best storytellers don't show off their vocabulary. They borrow yours.
Principle 06

Speak their
language

→
Use terms they already use
Figma, design tokens, component libraries, user journeys — whatever lives in their daily vocabulary. It signals you're one of them.
→
Reference designers they admire
"Similar to how Linear approaches motion..." or "Like the Duolingo team does with empty states..." Abstract ideas become concrete fast when grounded in work they know.
→
Avoid the jargon trap
If you're not sure they know a term, define it briefly, without condescension. Shared language builds connection. Exclusive jargon builds walls.
